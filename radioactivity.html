Radioactivity

An introduction to radioactivity for O-Level Physics students, explaining radioactive decay, types of radiation, and their uses.

What is Radioactivity?

Radioactivity is the process by which unstable atomic nuclei lose energy by emitting radiation. This happens naturally in certain elements such as uranium and radium.

Types of Radiation

Alpha (α) Radiation

Alpha particles consist of two protons and two neutrons. They are highly ionising but have a very short range. Alpha radiation can be stopped by a sheet of paper.

Beta (β) Radiation

Beta particles are fast-moving electrons. They are moderately ionising and can travel further than alpha particles. Beta radiation can be stopped by a thin sheet of aluminium.

Gamma (γ) Radiation

Gamma rays are electromagnetic waves with very high energy. They are weakly ionising but highly penetrating. Thick lead or concrete is needed to reduce gamma radiation.

Radioactive Decay

Radioactive decay is a random process in which an unstable nucleus emits radiation and changes into a different nucleus. During decay, the number of protons or neutrons in the nucleus may change.

The time taken for half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay is called the half-life. Half-life is a fixed property of a radioactive substance.

Uses and Dangers of Radioactivity

Uses

  • Medical treatment (radiotherapy for cancer)
  • Medical tracers
  • Smoke detectors
  • Carbon dating

Dangers

  • Can damage living cells
  • May cause mutations or cancer
  • Requires careful handling and shielding
